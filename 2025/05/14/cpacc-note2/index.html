CPACC Study Notes: Theoretical Models of Disability

Overview

In accessibility and disability studies, theoretical models offer different perspectives on what disability is and how society should respond to it. Understanding these models is critical for CPACC certification and real-world accessibility practice.

1. Medical Model

  • Definition: Views disability as a problem of the individual’s body or mind that needs to be cured or managed through medical treatment.
  • Example: A person using a wheelchair can’t enter a building due to stairs—the issue is seen as their inability to walk.
  • Strengths:
    • Recognizes medical needs.
    • Supports development of clinical treatments.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Ignores environmental barriers.
    • Can be disempowering.

2. Social Model

  • Definition: Sees disability as caused by an inaccessible society, not the person’s condition.
  • Example: That same person using a wheelchair is disabled by the lack of a ramp, not their body.
  • Strengths:
    • Focuses on changing environments and attitudes.
    • Empowers people with disabilities.
  • Weaknesses:
    • May underplay the importance of medical needs.

3. Biopsychosocial Model

  • Definition: Combines biological, psychological, and social factors to give a complete picture of disability.
  • Example: A person with chronic pain may have physical limitations, emotional distress, and face work challenges.
  • Strengths:
    • Holistic and balanced.
    • Useful in rehabilitation and care planning.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Complex to implement.
    • Risk of shifting focus too far from either medical or social realities.

4. Economic Model

  • Definition: Focuses on the economic impact of disability—on the individual, employer, and society.
  • Use Case: Often used to determine eligibility for disability benefits.
  • Strengths:
    • Highlights the need for financial and workplace accommodations.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Can be stigmatizing.
    • May exclude those who don’t meet strict legal definitions.

5. Functional Solutions Model

  • Definition: Focuses on solving practical problems caused by functional limitations, often through technology or design.
  • Example: Using voice recognition software for someone with limited hand movement.
  • Strengths:
    • Action-oriented and practical.
  • Weaknesses:
    • May overemphasize tech solutions without addressing systemic issues.

6. Social Identity / Cultural Affiliation Model

  • Definition: Views disability as part of a person’s identity and culture—most clearly seen in Deaf culture.
  • Strengths:
    • Fosters pride and community.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Can exclude those who don’t identify with a specific group.

7. Charity Model

  • Definition: Sees people with disabilities as objects of pity who need help from others.
  • Strengths:
    • Can motivate people to provide aid in emergencies.
  • Weaknesses:
    • Disempowering.
    • Often leads to short-term rather than systemic solutions.
