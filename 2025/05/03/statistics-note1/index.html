Statistical Reporting in User Research

Basic Statistical Terms

Mean and Standard Deviation

  • M (Mean): The arithmetic average of a group of values.
  • SD (Standard Deviation): A measure of how spread out the numbers are from the mean. A low SD indicates values are close to the mean, while a high SD indicates more variation.

Likert Scale

  • A psychometric scale commonly used in questionnaires.
  • Usually ranges from 1 (Strongly Disagree) to 7 (Strongly Agree).

Parametric Statistics (参数统计)

(Repeated-Measures) ANOVA

  • Format: F(df_between, df_within) = value

In a dependennt sample, the same test units(persons,animals..) are measured several times under different conditions(time points,treatments).

  • F: The F-statistic measures the ratio of variance between groups to the variance within groups.
  • p-value: Indicates the probability that the observed result is due to chance.
    • A p < 0.05 suggests statistical significance.
  • r (Effect Size): Measures the magnitude of the difference.
    • Small: ~0.1
    • Medium: ~0.3
    • Large: ~0.5+

📘 Common Statistical Terms and Methods in User Research

📊 Key Statistical Indicators

1. F-statistic (F)

  • Used in: ANOVA (Analysis of Variance)

  • Purpose: To test if there are statistically significant differences between group means.

  • Formula:
    F = variance between group means / variance within the groups

  • Example:
    F(1,15) = 10.024

    • The first number (1) is the between-groups degrees of freedom (df between)
    • The second number (15) is the within-groups degrees of freedom (df within)
    • 10.024 is the F-value — the larger it is, the more likely the group means differ.

2. p-value (p)

  • Purpose: Measures how likely it is that the observed results happened by chance.

  • Threshold:

    • If p < 0.05, the result is considered statistically significant.
    • If p < 0.01, it’s highly significant.

  • Example:
    p = 0.006 means there’s only a 0.6% chance the result is due to random variation.

3. r (Effect Size)

  • Purpose: Describes the strength or magnitude of an effect or relationship.

  • Common thresholds:

    • 0.1 = small effect
    • 0.3 = medium effect
    • 0.5 or higher = large effect

  • Example:
    r = 0.633 is a large effect, meaning the observed difference is not only statistically significant but also practically meaningful.

4. M (Mean) and SD (Standard Deviation)

  • M (Mean): The average value of a dataset.

  • SD (Standard Deviation): Measures how spread out the data is from the mean.

  • Example:
    If valence scores are M = 7.47, SD = 1.01, it means:

    • The average score is 7.47
    • Most scores fall within ±1.01 of the mean (6.46 to 8.48)

5. H-statistic (H) — Kruskal–Wallis Test

  • Used in: Non-parametric tests for comparing 3 or more independent groups.
  • Purpose: Like ANOVA, but for non-normally distributed data.
  • Example:
    H(3) = 29.240
    • 3 = degrees of freedom = number of groups – 1
    • 29.240 is the test statistic

6. U-statistic (Mann–Whitney U test)

  • Used for: Comparing two independent groups (non-parametric version of a t-test)

  • Purpose: Test whether the distributions of two groups are significantly different.

  • Example:
    U = 25.656, p < 0.001 → One group significantly outperforms the other.

🧪 Experimental Designs

7. Repeated-Measures Factorial Design

  • Purpose: All participants go through all conditions of the experiment.
  • Advantage: Controls for individual differences by using the same participants across conditions.
  • Example:
    A 2×2×2 design (System × HMD × Experience):
    • System: ShareVR vs. Baseline
    • HMD: HMD user vs. Non-HMD user
    • Experience: Game A vs. Game B

8. Latin Square Design

  • Purpose: To counterbalance the order of conditions and reduce order effects (fatigue, learning, etc.)
  • How it works: Each condition appears exactly once in each row and column.
  • Example (4 conditions):

$$
\begin{matrix}
A & B & C & D \

B & C & D & A \

C & D & A & B \

D & A & B & C
\end{matrix}
$$

Each participant pair follows a different row’s order.

🧮 Non-parametric Methods

9. Aligned Rank Transform (ART)

  • Purpose: Allows ANOVA-style tests on non-parametric data.
  • Why use: For factorial designs where assumptions like normality and equal variance are not met.
  • Benefit: Keeps the flexibility of ANOVA but suitable for non-normal data.

10. Kruskal–Wallis Test

  • Used for: 3 or more independent groups, non-parametric version of ANOVA.
  • Input: Ranked data instead of raw scores.
  • If significant: You need a post-hoc test to find out which groups differ.

11. Dunn–Bonferroni Post-Hoc Test

  • Used after: A significant Kruskal–Wallis test.

  • Purpose: Compares each group pairwise, while correcting for multiple comparisons using the Bonferroni correction.

  • How: Adjusts the significance level (α / number of comparisons)

  • Example:
    You compare 4 groups. That’s 6 comparisons. Bonferroni correction sets the new p-threshold at 0.05 / 6 = 0.0083.

🎮 Summary of Standardized Questionnaires for User Experience in Virtual Environments

These widely-used questionnaires help researchers systematically evaluate gameplay experiences, emotional responses, and the feeling of presence in virtual or shared environments. Here’s a breakdown of the most common tools.

🧩 1. Game Experience Questionnaire (GEQ)

The Game Experience Questionnaire (GEQ) is a comprehensive tool for measuring how players feel during and after gameplay. It captures both emotional reactions and cognitive involvement through several modules.

🔹 Core Module

Assesses the immediate personal experience during gameplay, typically using a 5-point Likert scale (e.g., from “Not at all” to “Extremely”).

Subscale What It Measures Example Item
🎉 Positive Experience Enjoyment, flow, and emotional engagement “I felt happy while playing.”
😠 Negative Experience Frustration, annoyance, or feeling lost “I felt frustrated during the game.”
😩 Tiredness Physical or mental fatigue caused by the experience “I felt exhausted after the game.”
🌐 Returning to Reality Difficulty re-adjusting to the real world after immersive play “I needed time to adjust after the game.”

🔹 Social Presence Module (Optional)

Focuses on interpersonal dynamics in multiplayer or co-experience settings.

Subscale Description Example Item
🤝 Behavioural Involvement How much attention a player gives to their co-player(s) “I closely followed what the other person did.”
💓 Empathy & Emotional Contagion Emotional resonance and feeling what the other feels “I felt connected to the other person emotionally.”

💡 Use Case: The GEQ is ideal for measuring moment-to-moment and retrospective gameplay experiences across single or multiplayer settings.

😊 2. Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM)

The Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM) is a quick, non-verbal, and intuitive tool to measure users’ emotional states in response to stimuli (games, media, VR, etc.).

📏 Dimensions

Participants select a cartoon-like figure on a scale for each dimension.

Dimension Description Example
💖 Valence From unpleasant (sad face) to pleasant (happy face) 😠 😐 😊
⚡ Arousal From calm (low energy) to excited (high energy) 😴 😐 😲
🕹️ Dominance (Optional) From feeling dominated to in control 🧍⬇️ 🧍 🧍⬆️

🧠 Insight: SAM is useful when you want a lightweight emotional assessment — especially when working with children, cross-cultural users, or those unfamiliar with Likert scales.

🌍 3. Slater, Usoh, and Steed (SUS) Presence Questionnaire

The SUS Presence Questionnaire is specifically designed to measure how “present” or immersed someone feels in a virtual environment (VE).

🧠 Psychological Components

Component What It Reflects Sample Item
📍 Place Illusion Sense of “being there” physically “I felt I was really inside the virtual environment.”
🧠 Plausibility Illusion How real the scenario feels mentally “The scenario seemed like it could really happen.”
👁️ Attention/Involvement Focus level in the VE, ignoring real-world distractions “I concentrated entirely on the virtual experience.”

🔎 Insight: SUS helps researchers understand presence as both a sensory and psychological phenomenon, making it essential for VR studies.

📊 Comparison Table

Questionnaire What It Measures Format/Scale Type
GEQ (Core) Positive/negative experience, tiredness, reality return Likert scale (e.g., 1–5)
GEQ – Social Presence Social interaction, attention, emotional connection Likert scale (e.g., 1–5)
SAM Valence, arousal, (optional: dominance) Pictorial (Manikin) scale
SUS Presence Presence: place illusion, plausibility, involvement Likert scale (e.g., 1–7)

🧭 When to Use What?

  • 🕹️ GEQ: In gameplay studies to assess engagement, fun, and player fatigue.
  • 😃 SAM: For quick emotional feedback, especially in media or UX studies.
  • 🕶️ SUS: When evaluating presence in VR, e.g., comparing headsets or systems.
